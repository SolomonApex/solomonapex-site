Step
01

The One-Sentence Offer

Vague offers do not sell. Specific ones do.

Most people describe what they do in a way that sounds broad and forgettable. "I help businesses grow." "I do coaching and consulting." The problem is not that the work is bad. The problem is that the description fits everyone, so it speaks to no one.

A specific offer creates immediate clarity. When the right person hears it, they recognize themselves in it. That recognition is what moves someone from passive to interested.

The Framework
"I help [specific person] do [specific thing] so they can [specific outcome]."
Too Vague
Specific and Clear
"I help businesses improve their marketing."
"I help solo consultants book 3 new clients per month without running paid ads."
"I help people get their finances together."
"I help freelancers build a $5,000 emergency fund in 12 months using a weekly system."
"I help coaches build their online business."
"I help new coaches publish their first 10 pieces of content and book their first paid client."

Step
02

The Right Person

You cannot serve everyone. The clearer your buyer, the easier the sale.

"Small business owners" is not a buyer. It is a population of millions of people with different industries, budgets, problems, and goals. An offer built for all of them is built for none of them. The more precisely you can describe who you are for, the more it resonates with exactly that person.

Answer these five questions about your buyer:

  • 1
    What is their specific situation right now?Job title, life stage, what they do day to day, where they are stuck.
  • 2
    What problem are they actively trying to solve?Not a general frustration. A specific problem they think about this week.
  • 3
    What have they already tried that did not work?This tells you where they are in the buying journey and what to avoid saying.
  • 4
    What does success look like for them in 90 days?Be specific. A number, a feeling, a completed thing.
  • 5
    What are they afraid will happen if they do not solve this?Fear is one of the clearest signals that a buyer is ready to act.
The Watering Hole Test
Can you name three specific places this person spends time? A Facebook group. A specific subreddit. A podcast they listen to every week. A newsletter. A conference. If you can name three, you know your buyer. If you cannot, you need to keep narrowing.

Step
03

The Price

Start with the outcome. Work backwards to a number.

Most people price based on their comfort level rather than the value of the result. Start with this instead: what is the outcome worth to the buyer? If a consultant helps a freelancer land two new clients who pay $3,000 each, that outcome is worth $6,000. Charging $300 for it signals low confidence in the result. The price should reflect what the buyer gains, not how long you spend.

The framework: Estimate the concrete value of the outcome for your buyer. Set your price at 10–20% of that value as a reasonable starting point for a new offer. Adjust from there based on proof, demand, and what the market confirms.

Mistake One
Pricing Too Low

Low prices signal low confidence. They attract buyers who question every detail and resist the process. They also create resentment in the person delivering the work. A price that feels too low will erode the relationship before it starts.

Mistake Two
Pricing High Without Clarity

A high price with a vague outcome breaks trust immediately. The buyer cannot see what they are getting, so they default to skepticism. The answer is not to lower the price. It is to sharpen the result you are promising.

Three questions to test your price before you use it:

  • If I charged this price, would I resent doing the work? (If yes, charge more.)
  • Does this price reflect the outcome the buyer gets, not just my hours? (It should.)
  • Would a version of my ideal buyer find this reasonable given what they are getting? (It should feel fair, not cheap.)

Step
05

The Gut Check

Five questions that reveal if your offer is real or still wishful thinking.

You now have a written offer. Before you try to sell it, answer these five questions honestly. A yes to all five means you are ready to start having real conversations. A no to any of them means there is one more piece of work to do first.

  • Can you describe your ideal buyer in one specific sentence?
    Not a category. A person. If you could not describe them to a stranger in 15 seconds, keep refining.
  • Have you talked to at least one real person who fits this description in the last 30 days?
    Not surveyed. Not posted in a group. Had an actual conversation about their problem.
  • Have you said your price out loud to another person?
    If you have only typed it, you have not fully committed to it. Say it out loud once before your first real conversation.
  • Do you know exactly what problem this offer solves and why that problem matters to the buyer?
    If you cannot answer this in one sentence, go back to Step 2.
  • Could you deliver a version of this offer for the right person who hired you today?
    Not perfectly. But actually. If the answer is no, the offer is still a concept. Work it down until it becomes real.

If you answered NO to any of these: that question is your next task. One specific answer to one specific gap. Do not move forward until you can check every box.

The One Question That Matters Most
"Have you described this offer to a real person and gotten a real response?"
Not sent a DM. Not posted. Talked. An offer that has never been spoken out loud is still a theory. One real conversation will tell you more than a week of refining the document alone. That conversation is the next step.
